## Intro
Short answer: for most standard wellness drips at a private IV clinic in
Canada, you do not need a referral from your family doctor the way you would
for a specialist. But that does not mean anyone can walk in and get anything.
IV therapy is a medical act, and the clinic itself is responsible for making
sure a qualified professional assesses you and authorizes your drip. This guide
explains how that works, when a prescriber is genuinely required, and the
questions that tell you a clinic is doing it properly.

## The difference between a referral and a prescription
These get used interchangeably, but they are not the same thing.
- A **referral** is one clinician sending you to another (usually to a
  specialist). Private IV clinics are direct-access, so you almost never need a
  referral from your GP to book.
- A **prescription or medical order** is a qualified prescriber authorizing a
  specific treatment for you. For IV therapy this is often handled inside the
  clinic: a nurse practitioner, physician, or (within their provincial scope) a
  naturopathic doctor reviews your intake and authorizes the drip. You may
  never hold a paper prescription, but the authorization still has to happen.

## How intake usually works at a Canadian IV clinic
1. You book (many clinics take walk-ins; some require an appointment).
2. You complete a health intake form: medications, conditions, allergies,
   pregnancy, kidney and liver history, and your goal for the visit.
3. A qualified professional reviews it. Depending on the clinic and your
   history, this is a nurse under medical oversight, a nurse practitioner, a
   physician, or a naturopathic doctor.
4. They confirm the drip is appropriate, or adjust or decline it. A good clinic
   will say no when something is not safe for you.
5. A regulated professional places the IV and monitors you during the infusion.

## When a prescriber is genuinely required
Some things are not "wellness drips" and require a prescriber's order and often
bloodwork first:
- **Iron infusions** usually require recent bloodwork (ferritin, iron studies)
  and a prescriber's order, because dosing depends on your labs.
- **Prescription medications added to a drip** (certain anti-nausea meds, high
  doses, etc.) require a prescriber.
- **Anything for a diagnosed medical condition**, as opposed to general
  wellness, belongs under a prescriber's care.
If a clinic offers an iron infusion or a prescription add-on with no intake, no
bloodwork, and no prescriber involved, treat that as a red flag.

## Who can authorize and place your IV (varies by province)
Rules are set provincially, and scope of practice differs. In general, IVs are
started by a regulated health professional such as a registered nurse, nurse
practitioner, or physician, and in some provinces a naturopathic doctor within
their scope, under medical oversight. We keep a province-by-province breakdown
here: /blog/who-can-legally-give-iv-canada-rules-by-province-2026.

## What to ask before you book
- Who reviews my intake, and who places the IV? (Look for a regulated title.)
- Is there medical oversight, and who provides it?
- For an iron infusion: do you require bloodwork and a prescriber's order first?
- Where do your IV ingredients come from? (A licensed compounding pharmacy or
  prepared on site is what you want to hear.)
Clinics that answer these clearly are the ones worth booking. On TheDripMap,
clinics that confirm these in writing carry a Safety Verified badge.

## FAQs
**Do I need my family doctor's referral for an IV drip in Canada?**
Usually no. Private IV clinics are direct-access. You book directly, complete an
intake, and a qualified professional at the clinic authorizes the drip.

**Do I need a prescription for a vitamin drip?**
For a standard wellness vitamin drip, the authorization is typically handled
inside the clinic after your intake. For iron infusions or prescription add-ons,
a prescriber's order (and often bloodwork) is required.

**Can I just walk in?**
Many clinics accept walk-ins for standard drips, but you still complete a health
intake and can be declined if a drip is not appropriate for you.

**Is a naturopath allowed to give IV therapy?**
In several provinces, naturopathic doctors administer IV therapy within their
regulated scope. It varies by province; confirm with the clinic and see our
province guide.
